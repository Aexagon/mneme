The short version

What it actually does for you

Think of Mneme as a good assistant’s memory. Tell Claude something durable — how you like things, a fact about your business, a preference — and it holds onto it. The next time you open a chat, it already knows, so you stop repeating yourself.

It runs quietly in the background. There is nothing to set up after installing, and nothing to remember to do.

How it works

A simple loop, and you barely touch it

You talk

Chat with Claude exactly like normal — no special words, no commands.

It notices

When you say something worth keeping, Claude quietly makes a note of it.

You okay it

Every so often it asks, in plain words, “want me to keep these?” You say yes to the good ones.

It carries over

Every new chat already knows — and the loop begins again.

The one habit worth having: when Claude offers to fold notes into memory, glance and say yes to the keepers. That’s the whole maintenance.

How to use it

Just say it out loud

You never type a command. You talk, and Claude does the right thing. A few of the things it listens for:

Remember that I prefer short, bullet-point replies.
Claude saves it and confirms. Next chat, it already answers that way.
What do you know about my business?
It tells you everything it has remembered about you.
Forget what I said about the old logo.
It finds that note and removes it.
Tidy up your memory.
It clears out duplicates and anything out of date — checking with you before it deletes.

Two kinds of memory

Everyday facts, and the reference shelf

Mneme keeps two kinds of memory. You don’t have to manage the difference — but knowing it helps you understand what to expect.

Everyday memory

The things you’d just tell someone

Your preferences, facts about your work, how you like things done. Small, durable notes that Claude carries into every chat automatically, so it always has your context on hand.

When to use it: anything you’d say in a sentence. Just tell Claude — “remember that…”
Reference shelf

Whole documents to consult

Bigger material you want Claude to be able to look at — brand guidelines, a stack of research, a long transcript. It’s kept on the side and opened only when it’s relevant, so it never clutters your everyday chats.

When to use it: a whole file or a big body of reference. Ask Claude to “add this document to your knowledge.”

Getting started

Installing takes about a minute

You do this once. If you can copy and paste, you can install Mneme.

1

Open Claude Code

That’s the app where you type to Claude.

2

Paste these two lines, one at a time

Type or paste each line, press enter, then do the next.

/plugin marketplace add Aexagon/mneme /plugin install mneme@mneme
3

Restart, and you’re done

Close and reopen the session. You’ll see a small “Mneme is active” note at the top of your chats. From then on it just works.

Nothing to configure. Nothing to remember. Just talk to Claude.

What to expect

The first couple of weeks

  • Week 1It feels like normal Claude. Behind the scenes it’s quietly taking notes as you work.
  • Week 2+Chats start already knowing your context. You notice yourself repeating things less and less.
  • OngoingNow and then Claude offers to fold its notes into memory. Glance and say yes to the useful ones. That’s the only habit.
Your memory stays yours. Everything Mneme remembers lives on your own computer, as plain text files. Nothing is sent anywhere, and you can read or delete any of it at any time. If you ever want a clean slate, just ask Claude to clear its memory.
For the curious: the commands underneath you never need these
/mneme:rememberSave something on the spot.
/mneme:recallSearch your memory directly.
/mneme:reviewGo through the notes Claude jotted down.
/mneme:statusA quick health check of your memory.
/mneme:ingestAdd a document to the reference shelf.
/mneme:lintFind stale or broken notes.
/mneme:loopHave Claude drive a goal to completion.

Everything on this list also happens for you just by talking. The commands are shortcuts, not the front door.
